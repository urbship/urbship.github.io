We went to England this year for our vacation. Below are some of the pictures of that trip. Captions are mostly placeholders put in by Dave - Rachel's contributions are in blue.
2 September
On our first day in England, we went to Brighton. This is the famous Royal Pavilion. The Prince Regent, later George IV, had this built between 1815 and 1822, more or less as a party palace. It was mostly covered in scafolding when I lived there, so it was nice to see it.
On the beach at Brighton, with the Brighton Pier in the background.
3 September
We went for a hike along the Sussex Downs, ending up at some windmills called Jack and Jill. This is Jill, which is still a working mill.
We had tea at the home of a classmate of mine from Sussex in 1986-87. Sylvia is second from left, and her husband Neil is next to her on the right. Another classmate of ours, Charles, on the left of the picture, came down from his home in Swindon to join us.

This is the squalid hovel in which I lived while attending University of Sussex. My window was the second one down on the left. It's really extraordinary how the builders of the 1960s thought it would be good to fill this beautiful valley with such an ugly collection of buildings.
Here are the Urban Shipleys in front of the ugly building where the Energy Studies program was headquartered.
Here is my classmate, Charles, who joined us to wander about the University of Sussex. It was fortunate that he was there, because his memory of the campus was much better than mine. He stayed on in England, and has been back to the University fairly regularly since we studied there together.
We had lunch under this wonderful, huge tree outside the Fishbourne Roman Palace. It was a very windy day, and we kept having to grab hold of the newspapers and plastic bags when they tried to run away.

12 September
Our first day in London, we took a boat down the Thames to Greenwich. It was fun, but Ben and I were worried that something would fall in the river.
The Cutty Sark, the only surviving tea clipper.
Half of each child is in the Eastern Hemisphere and the other half is in the Western Hemisphere.
This is the red ball that descends the pole at exactly 1 p.m. Greenwich time. It used to be used by mariners to set their ships' chronometers before a voyage. Dad managed to take a picture while the ball was going down.
This is what Dad really wanted to see at Greenwich. The National Maritime Museum had a replica of H1 actually running. This was the first clock capable of keeping accurate enough time during a long sea voyage for the navigator to be able to calculate longitude accurately.
This is the fourth of John Harrison's great clocks, H4. Unlike its predecessors, it can be easily carried in one hand. The original was on display. It is in working order, but they don't wind it up, because it would wear out.
That night we went out for supper at an Italian restaurant not far from our hotel, with an old friend of Ruth's, Arthur Fleiss, and his wife, Mary Williams.

14 September
The next day we went for a ride on the London Eye, the huge ferris wheel that was built for the Millenium celebrations. It is 135 metres high. There's a very nice view from the top.
Here's a view of the river from up there.
This is the view of Buckingham Palace. Don't think you get a very good view of the changing of the guard, unfortunately.
This is what the next pod along the wheel looks like when you are near the top.
Houses of Parliament.
We walked along the south bank of the Thames on our way from the London Eye to the Globe Theatre. People were preparing for an upcoming festival, and one of the things going on was sand sculpting. The sculptor was at work on this amazing thing as we went by.
We went to the Tempest at Shakespeare's Globe Theatre. I took a few pictures inside before the show started.
It's an amazing reconstruction, quite wonderful inside. The roofs are all thatch, and everything is made of wood. Except when a jet flies over, or when you look at people's clothes, you could almost imagine you were back in Elizabethan England.
The people who stand up through the show are called "groundlings" and they pay only five pounds a ticket. They're not allowed to bring umbrellas, so if it rains they need to have raincoats. No danger of that on this day, though. The haziness in this shot is because I was shooting into the sun (sorry about that). In fact, the weather was so warm and sunny that we got too hot sitting where we were. The groundlings are also not allowed to bring anything to sit on, and if they sit on the ground, ushers come and tell them to get up. At one point, the action in the play stopped when an actor became aware that someone standing near the front had fainted. He called for ushers to come and carry her out.
Here we are just before the play started. Unfortunately, the production was a bit avant garde for us. It was performed by three actors, a chorus and some dancers. The three actors each had to play many roles, switching fluidly from one to the next. I was able to follow it well, because I know the play quite well, but it was a bit challenging for the younger set. Fortunately, there was some good physical acting and some good comic bits that were well done, so we were all entertained.
I finally got a bit more done on the playhouse. Here's an update.
This photo shows the new ladder raising system. The ladder is in the raised position. Cables run over pulleys and down to the counterweights, which hang inside the tubes on the two corners. Yes, those are black plastic plumbing stacks. The weights themselves are old window sash counterweights my dad had. The ladder does not move on its own - it will stay in whatever position it is left. The weights are enough to hold it, but not enough to overcome friction. For photos of earlier stages of the playhouse construction, refer to last year's photo page.
